# Rachel's Photo Strategy Rules for Design Diagnosis

**Author:** Rachel (Interior Decorator + Stager)  
**Date:** 2026-04-01  
**Purpose:** Photo audit guidelines for Design Diagnosis app + TikTok content  
**Impact:** Photos are the first filter; bad photos = lost bookings even with good design

---

## The Three Core Rules

### Rule #1: Limit Listings to 30 Photos Maximum

**The Problem:**
- Delta Blue has 78 photos (unreasonable)
- Photo quality degrades toward the end (last photos are worst)
- Guests stop scrolling halfway through
- Too many photos = decision paralysis + confusion

**The Solution:**
- **Maximum 30 photos per listing**
- Curate only the BEST photos
- Every photo must serve a purpose
- First 10 photos = hero images (decision-makers)
- Photos 11–30 = detail shots + variety

**Why This Matters:**
- Guests scroll top-to-bottom in order
- If photo 15 is bad, they may not get to photo 20 (which is good)
- More photos = more opportunities for bad shots to appear
- Airbnb algorithm favours listings with "good photo quality metric"

**For Design Diagnosis App:**
- Flag listings with >30 photos
- Recommendation: "Curate to 30 best photos; remove the rest"
- Cost: $0 (just deletion/organization)
- Impact: HIGH (perceived quality + booking confidence)

**Scoring Impact:**
- 50+ photos = −2 points (perceived low-end, clutter mentality)
- 30–50 photos = 0 points (acceptable)
- <30 photos well-curated = +1 point (professional approach)

---

### Rule #2: Ensure Photo Consistency (No "Photo Variants")

**The Problem:**
- Delta Blue shows living room with grey sofa, then same room with blue sofa
- Same bedroom shown 3 times with different bedding
- Guest arrives expecting one thing, gets another
- This is a TRUST/CREDIBILITY KILLER

**The Solution:**
- **One configuration only**
- All photos show the SAME furniture, bedding, staging
- No "here's what it looks like with Option A" and "here's Option B"
- If you change something, reshoot everything with new configuration

**Critical Distinction:**
- ✅ GOOD: Multiple angles of the same bedroom (bed from door, bed from side, detail of bedside table)
- ❌ BAD: Same bedroom with different bedding in different photos
- ✅ GOOD: Living room from 3 angles (from entry, from kitchen, from sofa perspective)
- ❌ BAD: Living room with grey sofa in one photo, blue sofa in another

**Why This Matters:**
- Guests make booking decision based on photos
- If actual property differs from photos, guest feels deceived
- Inconsistency = automatic 1-star review ("doesn't match photos")
- This is THE #1 trust issue in short-term rentals

**For Design Diagnosis App:**
- Flag inconsistent photos (advanced: AI vision to detect different furniture in same room)
- Recommendation: "Restage entire property for consistency. Reshoot all photos."
- Cost: $0–500 (staging + photography)
- Impact: CRITICAL (trust + reviews)

**Scoring Impact:**
- Inconsistent photos = −5 points (credibility killer)
- Consistent photos = +2 points (professional)

**Example (Delta Blue):**
- Photo 12: Living room with grey sofa, bare windows, wooden table
- Photo 18: Same living room angle, but blue sofa, different table
- Guest books expecting blue (from photo 18), arrives to grey (from photo 12)
- Result: 1-star review "Doesn't match photos"

---

### Rule #3: No Close-Ups of Irrelevant Items

**The Problem:**
- Close-up of a tap/faucet (WHY?)
- Close-up of a single plant (distracting)
- Close-up of budget artwork (screams "cheap")
- Close-up of a light fixture (confusing)
- These waste valuable photo slots (remember: max 30)

**The Solution:**
- **Every photo must show context**
- No close-ups unless they're RELEVANT to the property's selling point
- Examples of GOOD close-ups:
  - Luxury bathroom fixture (if it's a feature)
  - Coffee maker (if it's a high-end espresso machine)
  - Shower head detail (if it's rainfall/luxury)
  - Bed detail (thread count, quilting, if premium)
- Examples of BAD close-ups:
  - Standard tap (who cares?)
  - Random plant (nobody books for the ficus)
  - Budget artwork (advertises your budget)
  - Generic light bulb (irrelevant)
  - Door handle (unless it's a statement piece)

**The Philosophy:**
Rachel: "They clearly need to learn how to take photos or hire a professional photographer."
- **Recommendation: Hire a professional photographer**
- Cost: $300–500 for a full shoot
- ROI: 15–25% increase in booking rate (industry data)
- This is not optional for premium properties

**For Design Diagnosis App:**
- Flag close-up photos that don't serve a purpose
- Recommendation: "Remove close-ups of standard fixtures. Hire photographer for professional shots."
- Cost: $300–500 (photographer)
- Impact: CRITICAL (perceived professionalism)

**Scoring Impact:**
- Poor photo composition = −2 to −3 points
- Professional photography = +2 points
- Budget close-ups = −1 point each

## The Photo Audit Checklist (For Design Diagnosis)

### Part 1: Photo Count
- [ ] 30 photos or fewer?
  - Yes → 0 points
  - No (31–50) → −1 point
  - No (50+) → −2 points

### Part 2: Consistency
- [ ] All photos show same furniture configuration?
  - Yes → +2 points
  - Mostly (1–2 variants) → 0 points
  - Multiple variants (3+) → −5 points
- [ ] All photos show same bedding/decor?
  - Yes → +1 point
  - Different in different rooms (acceptable) → 0 points
  - Same room, different bedding → −3 points

### Part 3: Photo Quality
- [ ] Professional composition (not blurry, good lighting)?
  - Yes → +1 point
  - Mostly → 0 points
  - Poor quality → −2 points
- [ ] No irrelevant close-ups?
  - Yes → +1 point
  - One or two → 0 points
  - Many (5+) → −2 points

### Part 4: Coverage
- [ ] All major spaces shown (entry, living, kitchen, bed, bath)?
  - Yes → +1 point
  - Missing spaces → −1 point

## Recommendations by Scenario

### Scenario A: Too Many Photos (50+)
**Recommendation:** Curate to 30 best photos
- **Step 1:** Review all photos in order
- **Step 2:** Delete clearly bad shots (blurry, poorly lit, irrelevant)
- **Step 3:** Keep only the photos you'd show a friend
- **Step 4:** Ensure remaining photos show consistent property
- **Step 5:** Reshoot if needed for consistency
- **Cost:** $0 (curation) to $300–500 (new photos)
- **Timeline:** 2–4 hours (curation) or 1–2 hours (photoshoot)

### Scenario B: Inconsistent Photos
**Recommendation:** Restage + Reshoot entire property
- **Step 1:** Choose ONE configuration (best version of each room)
- **Step 2:** Stage entire property to that standard
- **Step 3:** Hire professional photographer
- **Step 4:** Replace all old photos with new consistent set
- **Cost:** $500–1,000 (staging + photography)
- **Timeline:** 1–2 days (staging + shoot)
- **Impact:** +10–15 points on Vitality Score

### Scenario C: Poor Photo Quality (Generic, close-ups, bad lighting)
**Recommendation:** Hire professional photographer
- **Cost:** $300–500
- **Timeline:** 2–3 hours (shoot day)
- **Includes:** 30–40 professional photos (host curates to best 30)
- **ROI:** 15–25% booking increase
- **Impact:** +3–5 points on Vitality Score

## Integration into Design Diagnosis Scoring

### New Dimension: "Photo Strategy" (0–10 points)

This becomes a **6th dimension** in Vitality Score:

**Scoring Logic:**
- Photo count: 30 or fewer = +3 points (curated)
- Consistency: All same config = +4 points
- Quality: Professional = +2 points
- Relevance: No irrelevant close-ups = +1 point
- **Maximum: 10 points**

**Example:**
- Brown Town: Poor photos (inconsistent sofa + too many close-ups) = 3/10
- Hobby Lobby: Professional photos, 25 count, consistent = 9/10

**Impact on Total Vitality Score:**
- Old system: 100 points (5 dimensions × 20)
- New system: 110 points (5 × 20 + photo strategy × 10)
- Scale to 0–100 by dividing by 1.1
