## Where Rachel's Expertise Could Amplify the App

### TIER 1: CRITICAL (Before Friday Launch)

#### 1. **Psychological/Emotional Design Impact** 
**What the app currently misses:**
- Why a striped bed feels like "prison bunk" (psychological framing)
- Why "found items" feels cheap vs. "curated" feels intentional
- Why monochromatic + cold = institutional vs. warm + intentional = luxury

**What Rachel knows:**
- Guest psychology (what makes them feel welcome vs. trapped)
- Emotional triggers (colours, patterns, layouts that create feelings)
- The psychology of "expectation vs. reality" (pricing misalignment = anger)

**How to capture it:**
- [ ] Rachel writes: "Emotional Design Framework" 
  - "What makes guests feel loved vs. trapped?"
  - Patterns that trigger claustrophobia (striped bed, brick door)
  - Colours that trigger institutional feeling (all grey/white/black)
  - Details that signal "care" vs. "neglect" (rolled towels, paired pillows, etc.)
- [ ] App learns: Flag designs that trigger negative emotions, even if technically sound

**Example:** Lower Luxury at 56/100 is "technically adequate" but "emotionally incomplete" (no living room). Rachel's framework would flag this as psychological failure, not just functional gap.

---

#### 2. **Guest Expectations by Price Point**
**What the app currently misses:**
- What $60 guests expect vs. $200 guests
- Why 53/100 succeeds at $60 (Free Will) but fails at $200 (Rent Free)
- The "expectation multiplier" for each price tier

**What Rachel knows:**
- What amenities justify what prices (from 10+ years of staging)
- How guests mentally calculate "price = quality" (anchoring effect)
- Which fixes provide best ROI at each price point

**How to capture it:**
- [ ] Rachel writes: "Price-Adjusted Vitality Framework"
  - $0–$80/night: What's essential? (bed, bathroom, basic kitchen, clean)
  - $80–$150/night: What creates value? (lighting, comfort furniture, entry design)
  - $150–$250/night: What justifies premium? (luxury details, professional staging)
  - $250+/night: What's expected? (boutique finishes, high-end everything)
- [ ] App learns: "Your score is 53/100. At $60, that's a win. At $200, that's a fail. Recommend reposition to $125."

**Example:** Rent Free — app scored it, but didn't warn "this pricing is wrong." Rachel would flag immediately.

---

#### 3. **The "Hidden Friction" Inventory** (Non-Visual Issues)
**What the app can't see:**
- Linen quality (pilling, stains, thread count)
- Top sheet presence/absence
- Pillow firmness variety
- Towel count per guest
- Kitchen essentials (can opener, sharp knives, scissors)
- Bathroom supplies (plunger, drain catcher, hooks)
- Welcome basket
- Power bar locations
- Key copies

**What Rachel knows:**
- These invisible failures tank reviews ("No top sheet!" = 1 star)
- Guests notice what's MISSING more than what's present
- A $100 investment in linens/basics = +0.5 star rating bump

**How to capture it:**
- [ ] Rachel writes: "Hidden Friction Checklist for Design Diagnosis"
  - Pre-visit checklist (what hosts should verify before guest arrival)
  - Photo audit clues (can you see linen quality in photos?)
  - Common deficiencies by room
- [ ] App learns: Flag photos that suggest hidden friction
  - Worn-looking bedding → "Likely low thread count"
  - No visible bathroom supplies → "Missing amenity basket"
  - Bare kitchen counter → "Missing essentials?"

**Example:** Lower Luxury photos show minimal kitchen/bathroom supplies. Rachel's framework would flag: "Guests will complain about missing can opener, sharp knives, etc."

### TIER 2: HIGH VALUE (Weeks 2–4)

#### 4. **Room-by-Room "Conversation Starters"**
**Concept:** Instead of just "bedside table," Rachel writes why it matters + what guests notice.

**What Rachel knows:**
- Why paired nightstands matter (symmetry = calm, professional, intentional)
- Why coffee table books matter (they say "this host has taste")

**How to capture it:**

  - "Why this matters to guests"

**Example:** Instead of "Add side tables," app says "Add side tables so guests have surfaces for phone/book/water. Cramped surfaces = guest anxiety."

#### 5. **Regional/Cultural Design Preferences**
**What the app currently misses:**
- Design preferences vary by region (Toronto ≠ Calgary ≠ Vancouver)
- Cultural nuances (urban Toronto guests expect minimalist; rural BC guests expect cozy)
- Local style trends (what sells in Vancouver may not sell in Winnipeg)

**What Rachel knows:**
- Which colours work in which markets
- What Toronto guests expect vs. Calgary guests
- Regional competition analysis (what other $150 properties in Calgary look like)

**How to capture it:**
- [ ] Rachel writes: "Regional Design Preferences by Market"
  - Toronto: Minimalist, modern, industrial (expect cool tones)
  - Calgary: Warm, cozy, approachable (expect warm tones)
  - Vancouver: Nature-inspired, modern luxury (expect greens, natural materials)
  - etc.
- [ ] App learns: Adjust recommendations by region
  - Toronto property with warm colours → Flag as "underperforming for market"
  - Calgary property with all grey → Flag as "doesn't match regional preferences"

**Example:** Graydar (Calgary) is all grey. Rachel's regional framework would say "Calgary guests prefer warmth. This is fighting the market."

#### 6. **Tier 1/2/3 Fix Prioritization Logic**
**What the app currently does:**
- Lists top 3 fixes
- Assigns cost estimates
- Shows impact

**What Rachel could add:**
- **Sequencing:** Which fix to do FIRST (to unblock others)
- **Dependency chains:** "Do bedside tables before adding lamps"
- **Quick wins:** "Slipcover sofa (cheap, fast, big impact) before furniture replacement"
- **Synergies:** "If you add lamps, also add blackout curtains (guests sleep better, leave better reviews)"

**How to capture it:**
- [ ] Rachel writes: "Fix Sequencing Framework"
  - Entry design (first impression, low cost, high impact)
  - Bedroom comfort (sleep quality, reviews)
  - Living room functionality (guest time spent there)
  - Then aesthetic refinements
- [ ] App learns: Prioritize by impact-to-effort ratio, not just impact

**Example:** Delta Blue — App says "remove fridge, coordinate sofa, add lamps." Rachel would sequence: "Remove fridge FIRST (architectural), THEN slipcover sofa (styling), THEN add lamps (ambiance)."
