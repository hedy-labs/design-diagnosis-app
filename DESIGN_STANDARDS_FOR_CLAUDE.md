## **1. BEDROOM DESIGN STANDARDS (CRITICAL)**

### Non-Negotiable Essentials
Every functional bedroom MUST have:

- **Bedside Lamp per bed** (warm 2700K bulb)
  - Missing = -5 to -8 points (CRITICAL)
  - Task: Guests must be able to read in bed
  - Cold overhead light only = institutional

- **Nightstand or Side Table per bed**
  - Missing = -3 to -5 points
  - Function: Place for phone, water, book, lamp

- **Curtains or Blackout Shades**
  - Missing = -3 to -5 points
  - Function: Light control, privacy, acoustic dampening
  - Bare windows = cold, exposed feeling

- **Area Rug**
  - Missing = -2 to -3 points
  - Function: Acoustic dampening, warmth, visual grounding

- **Textiles (Throws, Pillows, Quality Bedding)**
  - Missing = -2 to -4 points
  - Function: Warmth, texture, intentionality
  - Bare-bones (white sheet only) = institutional

### Bonus Elements (Add Warmth)
- **Closet with adequate hangers** (8–12 per guest)
  - Present but inadequate = -1 point
  - Missing entirely = -2 points
  - Guest frustration: nowhere to hang clothes

- **Dresser** (bonus, not required)
  - Adds +1 point if present
  - Guest comfort: storage for folded items

- **Laundry Hamper** (bonus, not required)
  - Adds +1 point if present
  - Guest courtesy: designated place for soiled items

- **Small Chair or Bench** (bonus, not required)
  - Adds +1 to +2 points if present
  - Guest comfort: alternative seating, place to sit while dressing

### Bedroom Emotional Design Rule
**Formula: Bedside Lamp + Curtains + Textiles + Functional Anchors = Residential Warmth**

Without all four core elements, a bedroom scores institutional, no matter how nice the surfaces.

**Example:**
- Bare-bones bedroom (bed + overhead light + no curtains, textiles, or nightstand) = -8 points automatic
- Bedroom with lamps, curtains, rug, textiles + nightstand + chair = +5 to +8 points

## **2. LAYOUT DYSFUNCTION DETECTION (CRITICAL)**

Layout errors signal poor design planning. Flag any of these:

### Appliance Placement
- **Kitchen appliances belong in kitchen only**
  - Refrigerator in living room = -3 points (MAJOR FLAG)
  - Stove/oven visible from sleeping area = -2 points
  - Washer/dryer in bedroom = -2 points (unless explicitly separate laundry closet)

### TV Placement
- **TV should be wall-mounted or integrated into design**
  - TV in corner (not wall-mounted) = -2 points
  - TV off to side (awkward viewing angle) = -2 points
  - TV as afterthought (not focal point) = -1 point

### Furniture Crowding
- **Furniture should not extend into adjacent zones**
  - Sofa extends into kitchen = -2 points (no zone separation)
  - Coffee table blocks passage = -1 point
  - Furniture crowded = cramped feeling

### Dining/Gathering Space
- **Defined dining area required for multi-bedroom properties**
  - No dining table for 4+ guest property = -3 points
  - Bar seating is acceptable alternative = no penalty
  - Sofa only (no gathering space) = -2 points for family properties

### Spatial Flow
- **Clear, logical pathways**
  - Dead ends = -1 point
  - Unclear zones = -1 point
  - Good flow = no penalty

### Layout Dysfunction Flag Rules
- Each major dysfunction = -2 to -3 points
- Multiple dysfunctions compound (e.g., fridge + TV corner + no dining = -7 total)
- Note: Possibilities are infinite — teach Claude as new examples emerge

## **3. COLOR PALETTE THEORY (HELPFUL)**

Rachel's Interior Design Color Framework:

### The 60-30-10 Rule (Spatial Balance)

**60% Dominant Color:**
- Usually walls, large area rugs, or primary flooring
- Sets the overall "temperature" of the room
- Example: Beige walls = warm dominant

**30% Secondary Color:**
- Upholstery (sofas, chairs), window treatments, or accent wall
- Supports dominant color while adding depth
- Example: Navy sofa in beige room

**10% Accent Color:**
- The "pop" — used for throw pillows, artwork, lamps, small decorative objects
- Example: Rust-colored throw pillows

### Scoring Rule
- **Proper 60-30-10 adherence** = +2 to +3 points (Color Coherence bonus)
- **Violates ratio** (e.g., 40-40-20, no accent) = -1 to -2 points
- **Random colors, no ratio** = -3 to -5 points (see "No Solace" lavender chair)

---

### Color Temperature & Mood

**Warm Colors (Red, Orange, Yellow, Rust, Terracotta, Gold):**
- Effect: Inviting, cozy, high-energy, intimate
- Best for: Social spaces (dining rooms, kitchens, living rooms)
- Bedroom use: Accents only (not dominant)

**Cool Colors (Blue, Green, Violet, Sage, Teal):**
- Effect: Calm, relaxing, spacious, serene
- Best for: Bedrooms, bathrooms, meditation spaces
- Living room use: Only if intentional (moody/cozy effect)

**Neutrals (White, Beige, Gray, Taupe, Cream):**
- Effect: Visual palette cleanser, balance, sophistication
- Best for: Base/dominant color in almost every room
- Rule: Never use neutrals alone (looks sterile) — add warmth with accents

### Scoring Rule
- **Appropriate temperature for room function** = +1 to +2 points
- **Mismatched temperature** (e.g., all cool blues in social living room) = -1 point
- **No warmth in bedroom** (monochromatic cool only) = -3 to -5 points

---

### Light Reflection Value (LRV) — Color's Impact on Space

**High LRV (Whites, Pastels, Light Grays):**
- Reflects more light
- Makes small rooms feel larger and brighter
- Can feel cold if used alone
- Example: White walls, light gray accents

**Low LRV (Darks, Moody Tones, Deep Blues, Charcoal):**
- Absorbs light
- Makes large rooms feel intimate, dramatic, grounded
- Can feel oppressive if overdone
- Example: Dark gray walls in small bedroom = institutional

### Scoring Rule
- **LRV appropriate for room size** = no penalty
- **High LRV in small room** = +1 point (feels spacious)
- **Low LRV in small room without warm accents** = -2 to -3 points (feels cramped/cold)
- **Light basement room using only dark colors** = -2 points (exacerbates darkness)

---

### Material-Color Interaction

**Texture's Effect:**
- Same color looks different on different fabrics
- Velvet sofa (absorbs light) = color appears deeper, richer
- Silk pillow (reflects light) = color appears brighter, lighter
- Linen (semi-reflective) = natural, authentic appearance

**Natural vs. Artificial Light:**
- Colors shift throughout the day
- North-facing rooms = bluish/cool light (colors appear cooler)
- South-facing rooms = warm/golden light (colors appear warmer)
- Artificial light (bulbs) = varies by Kelvin temperature (2700K = warm yellow, 4000K+ = cool white)

### Scoring Rule
- **Material-color match is intentional** = +1 point (shows curation)
- **Mismatch (shiny fabric in matte palette)** = -1 point
- **Lighting shifts colors dramatically** = -1 point (suggest warm bulbs)

---

### Color Harmonies for Interiors

**Monochromatic Harmony:**
- Uses different shades/tints of one color (e.g., light blue, navy, slate blue)
- Effect: Sophisticated, serene, cohesive
- Risk: Can feel boring or cold if not layered with textures
- Example: All-blue bedroom with different fabric textures = +2 points
- Example: All-blue bedroom with no textiles = -3 points (cold)

**Analogous Harmony:**
- Colors next to each other on color wheel (e.g., Blue + Green + Teal)
- Effect: Natural, restful, harmonious
- Risk: Less dramatic, can feel "safe"
- Example: Navy sofa + sage curtains + teal accent pillows = +1 point

**Complementary Harmony:**
- High-contrast opposites (e.g., Blue + Orange, Navy + Rust, Sage + Rust)
- Effect: Vibrant, energetic, focal-point-driven
- Risk: Can overwhelm if not balanced (use 60-30-10 rule)
- Example: Navy sofa + rust throw pillows (proper ratio) = +2 points
- Example: Equal navy + rust (no hierarchy) = -1 point

### Scoring Rule
- **Intentional harmony (mono/analogous/complementary)** = +1 to +2 points (Color Coherence bonus)
- **Random color mixing** = -2 to -3 points
- **No harmony evident** = -1 to -2 points

---

### Spatial Correction with Color (Advanced)

**Raising Ceilings:**
- Paint ceiling lighter than walls (white or light gray)
- Effect: Draws eye upward, feels spacious
- Basement application: Light ceiling + warm walls = feels less bunker-like

**Shortening a Hallway (Making It Feel Less Tunnel-Like):**
- Paint end wall darker or warmer color
- Effect: Makes end "advance," shortens visual distance
- Basement hallway: Warm accent at end = less institutional feel

### Scoring Rule
- **Intentional spatial correction with color** = +1 to +2 points (shows design expertise)
- **No effort at spatial correction** (all same color, small room feels cramped) = no penalty yet, but note for future

## **4. ARTWORK PLACEMENT & HANGING STANDARDS (HELPFUL)**

### The "Eye-Level" Rule (The 57-Inch Standard)

**The Center Point:**
- Center of artwork (NOT the top of the frame) should be **57 to 60 inches from floor**
- This mimics professional gallery standards
- Measured from center of frame to floor

**Exceptions:**
- Very tall household: Can lean toward 60 inches
- Hallway-only viewing: Can use 60 inches
- Never go below 54 inches (too low) or above 66 inches (too high and disconnected)

### Scoring Rule
- **Artwork hung at proper eye level (57–60")** = no penalty (correct)
- **Artwork too high** (above 66") = -2 points (COMMON MISTAKE, flagged in "No Solace")
- **Artwork too low** (below 54") = -1 point (less common)
- **Multiple pieces at different heights** = -1 point (looks amateur)

---

### Furniture Anchoring (The 6-10 Inch Gap)

**Connection to Furniture:**
- Artwork must be anchored to furniture below it (sofa, console, headboard)
- Never float aimlessly on bare wall

**The Gap Rule:**
- Bottom of frame should sit **6 to 10 inches above top of furniture**
- Too close (less than 6") = cramped feeling
- Too far (more than 10") = visual disconnect, looks like mistake

### Scoring Rule
- **Artwork properly anchored to furniture (6–10" gap)** = +1 point (shows design knowledge)
- **Artwork floating on bare wall** = -1 point
- **Artwork too close to furniture** (less than 6") = -1 point (cramped)
- **Artwork too far from furniture** (more than 10") = -2 points (disconnected)

---

### Scaling & Proportions (The 2/3rds Rule)

**Width Balance:**
- Single artwork (or gallery cluster) should occupy **60% to 75% of the furniture width**
- Example: 60" sofa → artwork should be 36"–45" wide
- Example: 36" console table → artwork should be 22"–27" wide

**Small Art Error:**
- Too-small artwork on large wall = "visual loneliness"
- Solution: Gallery wall, larger mat/frame, or pair with larger piece
- Scoring: Small art on large wall = -2 points (suggests gallery wall or upsize)

**Large Art Error:**
- Too-large artwork on small wall = overwhelming
- Solution: Choose smaller piece or adjust room layout
- Scoring: Oversized art = -1 point (suggests right-sizing)

### Scoring Rule
- **Proper 60–75% width coverage** = +1 point (shows proportion sense)
- **Too small for wall** (less than 50%) = -2 points (suggests gallery wall)
- **Too large for wall** (more than 80%) = -1 point (suggests downsizing)
- **Wildly mismatched** = -3 points (suggests complete redo)

---

### Gallery Wall Mechanics

**Spacing Consistency:**
- Maintain tight, consistent gap between frames = **2 to 3 inches**
- Larger gaps (more than 4") = disjointed, amateurish
- Smaller gaps (less than 2") = cramped

**The "Single Unit" Concept:**
- Treat multi-picture group as ONE large single shape
- Center of entire group should hit the 57-inch eye-level mark
- All pieces should feel like intentional collection, not random hangings

### Scoring Rule
- **Gallery wall with consistent spacing (2–3")** = +1 to +2 points (intentional, professional)
- **Inconsistent spacing** = -1 point (looks amateur)
- **Gallery wall centered at 57"** = +2 points (professional execution)
- **Gallery wall scattered/uneven** = -2 points (looks like mistakes)

---

### Orientation & Room Flow (Advanced)

**Vertical vs. Horizontal Orientation:**
- **Vertical (Portrait) pieces** = Make low ceilings feel taller
  - Use in rooms with 8 ft or lower ceilings
  - Draws eye upward
- **Horizontal (Landscape) pieces** = Make narrow rooms feel wider
  - Use in long, narrow spaces
  - Stretches visual perception

**The "Visual Path" Principle:**
- Art should lead the eye toward focal points (fireplace, window, feature wall)
- Never position art to draw eye away from focal point
- Example: Art above sofa leads eye to that wall (good). Art in corner leads eye nowhere (bad).

### Lighting & Reflection

**Light Source Considerations:**
- **Avoid direct opposite window placement** (unless museum-grade non-reflective glass)
- Glare washes out artwork in photos and reduces visibility
- Natural light is best, but must be diffused (not direct reflection)

**Shadowing Issues:**
- Ensure overhead lighting doesn't cast harsh shadow of frame onto artwork itself
- Position overhead light to the side, not directly above
- Harsh shadows in photos = unprofessional appearance = -1 point

### Artwork Placement Red Flags (From "No Solace" Correction)

❌ **Artwork hung too high** (above 66")
- Flag: "Artwork placement too high, breaks furniture connection"
- Penalty: -2 points

❌ **Artwork random placement** (no anchor point to furniture)
- Flag: "Artwork floating on wall, no clear intent"
- Penalty: -2 points

❌ **Artwork not to scale** (too small for wall, too large for space)
- Flag: "Artwork scale mismatch with wall space"
- Penalty: -2 points

❌ **Gallery wall uneven spacing**
- Flag: "Gallery wall inconsistent, appears accidental"
- Penalty: -1 to -2 points

❌ **Visible glare or shadow on artwork in photos**
- Flag: "Photography/lighting issue affects artwork visibility"
- Penalty: -1 point

## **5. STAGING PROFESSIONALISM (HELPFUL)**

### Airbnb Staging Diagnosis Checklist
*Essential for removing friction and creating an aspirational story within 3-second first impression*

#### **1. The Living Area (The "First Impression")**

**DO: Create Vignettes**
- Group small objects with intention: book + plant + ceramic mug on coffee table
- Tells a story of a relaxing morning/sanctuary
- Creates "visual interest" without clutter
- Scoring: +1 point (shows curation)

**DON'T: Over-Clutter with Knick-Knacks**
- Surfaces should be 50% empty (allow guests space for their belongings: keys, phones, wallets)
- Knick-knacks = feels cluttered and claustrophobic
- Over-styling = -1 point (too precious, guests feel they can't touch anything)

**Bonus Flags:**
- Side tables visible with lamps: +1 point
- Secondary seating (accent chair): +1 point
- Uncluttered surfaces: +1 point

---

#### **2. The Bedroom (The "Hotel Standard")**

**DO: Use White Linens**
- White communicates "clean" better than any pattern
- Use "triple sheeting" method (sheet + duvet + top sheet) or duvet with textured throw at foot for depth
- Scoring: +1 to +2 points (signals quality, cleanliness, intentionality)

**DO: Symmetry is Key**
- Matching nightstands + lamps on both sides of bed
- Creates professional, balanced, intentional appearance
- Asymmetry = amateur hour
- Scoring: +1 point (symmetry bonus)

**DO: Hide Visible Wires**
- Cable management boxes or hide lamp cords behind furniture
- Visible wires in photos = unfinished, messy appearance
- Penalty: -1 point (professional oversight)

**DO: Provide Multiple Pillows**
- Minimum 2 pillows per guest (firm + soft)
- Stacked or arranged intentionally
- Scoring: +1 point (comfort signal)

**DON'T: Leave Beds Bare or Unmade**
- Bare mattress = hospital or dorm feel
- Unmade bed = lacks intentionality
- Penalty: -2 points

---

#### **3. The Bathroom (The "Spa Feel")**

**DO: Display Fresh Towels**
- Rolled or neatly folded white towels on counter or in basket
- Spa psychology: signals abundance, cleanliness, luxury
- Scoring: +1 point (spa vibe, luxury signal)

**DO: Use Unified Dispensers**
- Replace mismatched soap/shampoo bottles with glass or matte plastic refillable dispensers
- Unified dispensers = high-end, curated, intentional
- Scoring: +1 point (attention to detail)

**DO: Arrange Supplies Intentionally**
- Fresh washcloths folded neatly
- Extra toilet paper visible (signals abundance)
- Bath mat in coordinating color
- Scoring: +1 point (professionalism)

**DON'T: Leave Toilet Lid Up in Photos**
- Toilet seat up = MASSIVE perception issue (cleanliness, carelessness, unprofessionalism)
- Penalty: -1 point (professional no-no)

**DON'T: Show Daily Clutter**
- No toothbrushes, medications, personal items
- No soap scum, mildew, or stains visible
- Penalty: -1 to -2 points

---

#### **4. Lighting & Ambiance (Critical for Photos)**

**DO: Layer Your Lighting**
- Turn on ALL lamps and overhead lights for photos
- Ensure consistent warm white bulbs (2700K to 3000K) throughout
- Mixing cool and warm bulbs = sickly, unprofessional appearance in photos
- Scoring: +1 to +2 points (professional lighting)

**DO: Use Natural Light**
- Combine window light with warm interior lamps
- Open curtains to show natural light
- Avoid harsh direct sun (use sheer curtains if needed)
- Scoring: +1 point (brightness, freshness)

**DON'T: Rely on Camera Flash**
- Flash flattens the room
- Creates harsh shadows
- Washes out textures and colors
- Penalty: -1 point (unprofessional photography)

**DO: Ensure Consistent Color Temperature**
- All bulbs should be 2700K to 3000K (warm white)
- Mixing temperatures = cold, institutional feeling in photos
- Scoring: +1 point (warmth consistency)

---

#### **5. Kitchen & Dining**

**DO: Set the Scene**
- Bowl of fresh lemons, wooden cutting board with crusty bread, herbs in a vase
- Creates "life" and aspirational feeling
- Tells a story of comfort and abundance
- Scoring: +1 to +2 points (aspirational staging)

**DO: Show Well-Stocked Supplies**
- Visible coffee setup, tea selection, basic condiments
- Signals "host cares about your comfort"
- Scoring: +1 point (hospitality signal)

**DON'T: Leave Small Appliances on Counter**
- Toasters, blenders, air fryers, coffee makers cluttering counters
- Makes kitchen look cramped and cluttered
- Clear the decks to make kitchen look larger and functional
- Penalty: -1 point (clutter)

**DON'T: Show Messy Dining Area**
- Chairs should be pushed in neatly
- No crumbs, spills, or clutter on table
- Dining table should be clear (or set with intention)
- Penalty: -1 point

---

### Photography Standards (General)

**Toilet Seat Position:**
- ALWAYS down in photos (professional standard)
- Toilet seat up = -1 point (signals careless photography)

**Surfaces & Styling:**
- Counters should be styled or clear (not cluttered)
- No personal items visible (toothbrushes, medications)
- Shower/bathtub clean and styled (not showing daily clutter)

**Textiles Visibility:**
- Bedding should show quality (high thread count appearance)
- Pillows should be arranged intentionally (not flat/unused-looking)
- Throws/blankets should be visible (signals coziness)
- Bare surfaces = institutional

**Welcome Signals:**
- Welcome basket or amenities visible = +1 point
- Coffee/tea setup visible = +1 point
- Fresh flowers or greenery = +1 point
- Personal touches (local art, handwritten note) = +1 point

### Scoring Rule
- **Professional staging throughout** (vignettes, white linens, unified dispensers, warm lighting, scene-setting) = +3 to +5 points (Staging Integrity)
- **Major oversights** (toilet seat up, bare surfaces, no styling, visible clutter) = -2 to -3 points
- **Welcome signals present** (basket, coffee setup, flowers) = +1 to +2 points bonus
- **Bare-bones, no styling attempts** = -3 to -5 points (institutional appearance)

## **6. EMOTIONAL DESIGN PSYCHOLOGY (MOST HELPFUL)**

### Why Psychology Matters
Guest satisfaction = 40% cleanliness + 40% functionality + **80% emotional feel**. A property can be spotless and functional but emotionally "dead" and score low (see "No Solace" at 66/100 despite being clean/functional).

---

### 1. Biophilia (The Nature Connection)

**The Principle:**
Humans have an innate need to connect with nature. Spaces devoid of organic elements feel "dead," clinical, or institutional.

**What to Look For:**
- Live plants (not plastic)
- Natural wood grains and wood furniture
- Stone, marble, or natural textures
- Views to outside, windows, natural light
- Organic patterns (florals, botanical art)

**Scoring Rule:**
- **Rich biophilia** (multiple plants, natural wood, stone, natural light) = +2 to +3 points (Staging Integrity)
- **Minimal biophilia** (one plant or minimal natural elements) = no penalty
- **Zero biophilia** (plastic plants, all synthetic materials, no windows/natural light) = -2 points (feels clinical)

**Result:** Reduces heart rate and cortisol levels; creates sanctuary feeling.

---

### 2. Prospect and Refuge Theory

**The Principle:**
Humans feel safest when they have:
- **Refuge:** Protected back (solid wall, headboard, back cushion)
- **Prospect:** Clear view of surroundings (can see door, window, approach points)

**What to Look For:**
- Is the bed placed against a solid wall with a view of the door/window? (Good)
- Is the sofa facing toward the room entrance? (Good)
- Is furniture "floating" in center of room with exposed back? (Bad = anxiety)
- Can guests see all approach points to the space? (Good)

**Scoring Rule:**
- **Good prospect/refuge** (bed against wall, sofa facing entry, clear sightlines) = +1 point (Functional Anchors)
- **Poor prospect/refuge** (floating furniture, back exposed, unclear entry) = -2 points (Clutter & Space Flow)

**Result:** Eliminates low-level anxiety, promotes deep relaxation and sleep quality.

---

### 3. Neuro-Aesthetics: Curves vs. Angles

**The Principle:**
Sharp angles and jagged edges trigger the "fear" center of the brain (amygdala), while curves and soft edges signal safety.

**What to Look For:**
- Square/rectangular rooms (hard architecture) vs. rounded elements in decor
- Sharp table corners vs. rounded or curved coffee tables
- Angular furniture vs. soft, curved seating
- Jagged artwork vs. flowing, organic shapes

**Scoring Rule:**
- **Balanced curves and angles** (hard architecture softened with rounded pillows, arched mirrors, circular rugs) = +1 point (Staging Integrity)
- **All hard angles, no softening** (square room, rectangular furniture, angular art, no curves) = -1 point (feels institutional or harsh)
- **Excessive roundness** (everything curved, no structure) = no penalty, but unusual

**Result:** Softens vibe of room, makes it feel more approachable and comfortable.

---

### 4. Hygge & Sensory Layering

**The Principle:**
Comfort is multi-sensory. A room that looks good but feels cold/hard will fail emotionally.

**What to Look For:**
- Soft textures: throws, high-pile rugs, plush pillows
- Warm lighting (dimmable, warm bulbs)
- Natural materials (wood, stone, linen, cotton)
- Layered textures (not all smooth/shiny surfaces)
- Sensory richness (multiple textures, materials, temperatures)

**Scoring Rule:**
- **Rich sensory layering** (soft throw + high-pile rug + dimmable warm lighting + mixed textures) = +2 to +3 points (Staging Integrity)
- **Minimal sensory layering** (functional but sterile) = -1 to -2 points (feels cold)
- **Hard textures only** (all shiny, smooth, reflective surfaces) = -2 to -3 points (institutional, antiseptic)

**Result:** Creates "cozy" psychology, essential for high Airbnb ratings and repeat bookings. Guests rate emotionally, not just functionally.

### 5. Color Psychology (Emotional Impact)

**Source:** Rachel's expert framework validated against Color Psychology wheel

#### **GREEN**
**Positive Attributes:** Positive, nature, wealth, renewal, growth, balance
**Negative Attributes:** Sickness, envy, immaturity
- Effects: Nature connection, stress reduction, renewal, cognitive flexibility, calming without being boring
- Best for: Home offices (focus + renewal), living rooms (biophilia), bedrooms (calming)
- Scoring: +1 to +2 points (shows intentionality toward wellness)
- **Application:** Sage, emerald, olive as dominant or secondary colors

#### **BLUE**
**Positive Attributes:** Calm, reliable, peace, trustworthy, loyal
**Negative Attributes:** Coldness, unemotional, sadness, depressing
- Effects: Trust, stability, lowered blood pressure, calm, serene
- Best for: Bedrooms (sleep-inducing), bathrooms (spa feel), offices (focus)
- Scoring: +1 to +2 points (conscious, calming choice)
- **Warning:** All blue without warm accents = -1 point (feels cold, institutional)
- **Application:** Navy, teal, powder blue as dominant; pair with warm accents (rust, gold, cream)

#### **YELLOW**
**Positive Attributes:** Eye-catching, sunny, light, optimism, happiness
**Negative Attributes:** Cowardice, abrasive, irritational
- Effects: Optimism, energy, appetite, warmth, uplifting
- Best for: Breakfast nooks, kitchens, social spaces, entryways
- Scoring: +1 point (appetitive, welcoming)
- **Warning:** Bright yellow as dominant = -1 point (overstimulating); softer gold/honey = +1 point
- **Application:** Gold, honey, butter yellow in accents or sunny kitchens

#### **ORANGE**
**Positive Attributes:** Warmth, energetic, bold, excitement, enthusiasm
**Negative Attributes:** Frustration, loud, caution, frivolous
- Effects: Warmth, energy, enthusiasm, friendly
- Best for: Social spaces (dining rooms, kitchens), accent walls, entryways
- Scoring: +1 point as accent (energizing); -1 point as dominant (overstimulating)
- **Application:** Burnt orange, terracotta as accent or secondary color only

#### **RED**
**Positive Attributes:** Passion, exciting, action, bold, courageous
**Negative Attributes:** Aggressive, angry, negative, dangerous, evil
- Effects: Heart rate elevation, passion, energy, stimulation
- Best for: Accents ONLY (artwork, throw pillows, accent wall in dining room), NOT dominant in bedrooms
- Scoring: +1 point as accent (creates focal point); -2 points as dominant (too stimulating, prevents sleep)
- **Warning:** Red in bedroom = -2 points (contradicts rest/sleep goal)
- **Application:** Deep red, burgundy, wine red as tiny accents only

#### **PURPLE**
**Positive Attributes:** Royalty, mystical, creativity, imagination, wisdom
**Negative Attributes:** Arrogant, entitlement, pompous, pretentious
- Effects: Creativity, imagination, mystery, luxury (when deep), spiritual
- Best for: Creative spaces, accent walls, luxury properties
- Scoring: +1 point (intentional, sophisticated); -1 point if too bright/overwhelming
- **Warning:** Bright purple = -1 point (feels childish); deep plum/eggplant = +1 point (sophisticated)
- **Application:** Plum, eggplant, deep purple as accent; avoid bright/neon purple

#### **BLACK**
**Positive Attributes:** Mysterious, sophisticated, security, elegance
**Negative Attributes:** Death, evil, menacing, heavy, oppressive
- Effects: Sophistication, drama, formality, but can feel heavy or intimidating
- Best for: Accents ONLY (trim, furniture legs, artwork frames, accent furniture like chairs)
- Scoring: +1 point as accent (adds sophistication); -2 points as dominant (feels institutional, oppressive)
- **Warning:** All-black room = -3 points (institutional, prison-like); black furniture with warm colors = +1 point
- **Application:** Black as trim, furniture details, or accent pieces only

#### **GRAY**
**Positive Attributes:** Neutral, mature, quiet, balance, sophistication
**Negative Attributes:** Drab, non-emotional, depressing, boring
- Effects: Calm, sophistication, balance, neutrality
- Best for: Walls (neutral base), paired with warm accents
- Scoring: +1 point (neutral base); -1 point if gray + gray + gray (feels depressing without warmth)
- **Warning:** All gray without warm accents = -2 points (feels institutional, cold, like a hospital)

### Color Selection Tips for Design Diagnosis

#### **1. Set a Focal Point**
Design color approach around existing focal points (art, furniture, rugs):
- If artwork is rust/gold, build palette around it
- If feature wall exists, colors should complement it
- Focal-point-driven approach = +1 point (shows intentionality)

#### **2. The 60-30-10 Rule (Reinforced)**
**60% Dominant Color** = Walls, large areas, base (usually neutral or warm)  
**30% Secondary Color** = Upholstery, window treatments, secondary surfaces  
**10% Accent Color** = Pops, throw pillows, art, decorative objects

**Scoring:**
- Proper 60-30-10 adherence = +2 points (shows design knowledge)
- Violates ratio (e.g., 40-40-20) = -1 point
- No hierarchy visible = -2 points

#### **3. The Importance of Lighting in Color Selection**
**Colors shift dramatically under different lighting:**
- Same color looks different in natural light (north-facing = cool blue light) vs. artificial (2700K = warm yellow light)
- Professional approach: Test colors under multiple lighting conditions before deciding

**Scoring:**
- Colors appear intentional under multiple light conditions = +1 point
- Colors clash under natural light (one too cool, one too warm) = -1 point
- Obvious mismatch (warm bulbs + cool paint = jarring) = -1 point

**Action for Claude:** Flag if lighting and color temperature clash (e.g., cool gray walls + warm 2700K bulbs = creates visual tension)

#### **4. Color Theory Application**

**Visual Reference:** Three Primary Color Schemes (see color wheel diagrams below)

**COLOR WHEEL METHODOLOGY:**

1. **COMPLEMENTARY** (Line across wheel)
   - Opposite colors on color wheel
   - High contrast, vibrant, energetic
   - Examples: Blue ↔ Orange, Red ↔ Green, Yellow ↔ Purple

2. **TRIADIC** (Triangle on wheel)
   - Three colors evenly spaced (120° apart)
   - Vivid, bold, balanced contrast
   - Examples: Yellow-Red-Blue, Green-Orange-Purple
   - All three feel equal importance

3. **ANALOGOUS** (Adjacent wedges)
   - Colors next to each other on wheel
   - Harmonious, natural, cohesive
   - Examples: Blue-Green-Teal, Yellow-Orange-Red, Red-Purple-Magenta
   - Smooth transitions between colors

Interior designers must understand:

**Complementary Colors** (opposite on color wheel):
- High-contrast, vibrant palette
- Examples: Blue + Orange, Red + Green, Yellow + Purple
- Creates focal points, energizes space
- Use sparingly (as accents) to avoid visual chaos
- Scoring: +1 point if intentional accent; -1 to -2 points if accidental clash or overuse

**Analogous Colors** (adjacent on color wheel):
- Colors that sit next to each other: Blue-Green-Teal, Yellow-Orange-Red, Red-Red-Purple-Purple, Blue-Blue-Green-Green
- Creates harmonious, natural, restful feeling
- Best for whole-space palettes (less jarring than complementary)
- Example: Navy sofa + sage curtains + teal accent pillows = smooth, nature-inspired
- Scoring: +1 to +2 points (shows design knowledge, professional harmony)

**Triadic Colors** (equidistant on color wheel, drawing a triangle):
- Three colors evenly spaced: Yellow-Red-Blue, Green-Orange-Purple, etc.
- Creates vivid, bold palette with contrasting hues that complement well
- Advanced technique, complex to execute well
- Rarely seen in successful residential (risk of visual chaos)
- Usually avoided in Airbnb for this reason
- Scoring: +2 to +3 points if successfully executed; -2 to -3 points if creates visual chaos (too busy)

**Monochromatic** (one hue with variations):
- Single main hue + different shades (add black), tints (add white), tones (add gray)
- Creates subtle, sophisticated, unified look
- Example: Light blue walls + navy sofa + powder blue accents + charcoal details = monochromatic depth
- Feels intentional, curated, serene
- Risk: Can feel boring or cold without texture/pattern variation
- Scoring: +1 to +2 points if paired with texture (fabrics, rugs); -1 point if feels flat/one-dimensional

## **6. TEXTILES & FABRICS (CRITICAL)**

### Why Textiles Matter
Textiles are the fastest way to signal either "luxury" or "institutional." A room with quality fabrics feels curated; a room without fabrics feels sterile. Claude must understand that **textile absence = automatic penalty**.

---

### Fabric Types & Psychological Impact

#### **Premium Fabrics (Quality Signals)**

**Linen:**
- Feel: Soft, natural, luxurious, slightly textured
- Psychology: Signals "quality," "natural," "European sophistication"
- Best for: Bedding, curtains, upholstery in mid-to-luxury properties
- Scoring: Linen bedding/curtains = +1 to +2 points (luxury signal)

**Cotton (High Thread Count 300+):**
- Feel: Soft, breathable, clean
- Psychology: Signals "quality," "comfort," "care for guest"
- Best for: Bedding (essential), towels, pillows
- Scoring: High-thread-count cotton = +1 point (guest comfort signal)
- **Warning:** Low thread count (100–200) polyester-blend = -1 point (cheap feeling)

**Velvet:**
- Feel: Plush, luxurious, soft to touch
- Psychology: Signals "luxury," "indulgence," "depth"
- Best for: Accent pillows, small upholstery pieces
- Color consideration: Velvet absorbs light, makes color appear deeper/richer
- Scoring: Quality velvet accents = +1 point (luxury, sensory richness)

**Wool:**
- Feel: Warm, textured, natural
- Psychology: Signals "quality," "warmth," "natural materials"
- Best for: Throws, area rugs, upholstery
- Scoring: Wool throw/rug = +1 point (warmth, texture, luxury)

**Silk:**
- Feel: Smooth, luxurious, reflective
- Psychology: Signals "luxury," "formality," "elegance"
- Best for: Pillow covers, small accents (not full bedding — too slippery)
- Scoring: Silk accents = +1 point (luxury signal)

#### **Budget Fabrics (Institutional Signals)**

**Polyester (Thin/Cheap):**
- Feel: Plastic-y, slippery, synthetic
- Psychology: Signals "cheap," "hotel dorm," "institutional"
- Common in: Budget hotel linens, fast-fashion textiles
- Scoring: Visible polyester bedding/throws = -1 to -2 points (feels institutional)

**Microfiber:**
- Feel: Soft but synthetic, slippery
- Psychology: "Budget comfort," okay but not premium
- Scoring: Microfiber blankets = no penalty, but not bonus (neutral)

**Fleece (in wrong context):**
- Feel: Warm but cheap-feeling, plastic-y
- Psychology: "Budget," "utilitarian," not luxury
- Scoring: Fleece throw in luxury property = -1 point (wrong vibe)

## **7. SPATIAL SCALE & PROPORTION (CRITICAL)**

### Why Proportion Matters
A room can have perfect colors and lighting but feel "wrong" if furniture is undersized or oversized. Claude must understand scale relationships to avoid missing properties that feel cramped or abandoned.

---

### Furniture Scale to Room Size

#### **The Golden Ratio: Furniture to Room**

**Large Spaces (300+ sq ft):**
- Furniture should occupy 50–70% of visual space
- Under 30% = looks abandoned, scattered (guest feels lost)
- Over 80% = looks cramped, cluttered

**Medium Spaces (150–300 sq ft):**
- Furniture should occupy 60–80% of visual space
- Under 50% = looks sparse, institutional
- Over 85% = looks cramped, claustrophobic

**Small Spaces (under 150 sq ft):**
- Furniture should occupy 70–85% of visual space
- Under 60% = looks empty, abandoned
- Over 90% = feels suffocating

**Scoring Rule:**
- **Correct proportion for room size** = +1 point
- **Under-furnished (too much empty space)** = -1 to -2 points (feels institutional or abandoned)
- **Over-furnished (cramped)** = -1 to -2 points (feels claustrophobic)

#### **Bed to Bedroom Ratio**

**Rule of Thumb:**
- Bed should occupy 40–50% of bedroom floor space
- Allows 2–3 feet walking clearance on all sides

**Scoring:**
- **Proper spacing** (queen bed in 12x14 room) = +1 point
- **Too small bed in large room** (full bed in 20x20 room) = -1 point (feels lonely, under-furnished)
- **Too large bed in small room** (queen in 10x10 room) = -2 points (cramped, suffocating)

#### **Sofa to Living Room Ratio**

**Rule of Thumb:**
- Sofa should not extend beyond 50% of room length/width
- Coffee table should be 2–3 feet from sofa edge
- At least 3 feet from walls for passage

**Scoring:**
- **Proper placement** (sofa centered, clear sightlines) = +1 point
- **Sofa extends into kitchen** (blurs zones) = -2 points (cramped, no boundary)
- **Sofa too small for room** (loveseat in 20x20 living room) = -1 point (scattered, isolated)
- **Furniture floating** (not anchored to walls, back exposed) = -2 points (prospect/refuge violation)

---

### Negative Space & Breathing Room

**DO: Intentional Negative Space**
- 30–40% of room should be open/walkable
- Signals luxury, breathing room, intentionality
- Guest feels comfortable moving, not trapped
- Scoring: +1 point (spacious, intentional design)

**DON'T: Cramped, Cluttered**
- Furniture crowded, no clear pathways
- Negative space under 15%
- Guest feels confined, anxious
- Scoring: -2 points (claustrophobic)

---

### Visual Scale of Accessories

#### **Artwork Scale (Covered but Reinforcing)**
- Single artwork: 60–75% of furniture width
- Gallery wall: group should be cohesive single unit
- Too small on large wall = visual loneliness = -2 points
- Too large for furniture = overwhelming = -1 point

#### **Mirror Scale**
- Mirror should be proportional to wall (not tiny in huge wall)
- Large mirrors expand perception of space = +1 point
- Small, random mirror = -1 point (looks accidental)

#### **Lighting Scale**
- Lamp shade should be proportional to table
- Oversized lamp in small room = -1 point (dominates)
- Tiny lamp in large room = -1 point (invisible)
- Proper scale = no penalty (expected)

---

### Room-to-Room Proportion

#### **Entryway Scale**
- Entryway furniture (console, mirror, hooks) should match room size
- Large entryway with tiny console = -1 point (scattered)
- Small entryway with large console = -2 points (cramped entry)

#### **Kitchen to Living Room Ratio**
- If kitchen opens to living room, furniture shouldn't blur zones
- Sofa extending into kitchen = -2 points (no boundary, cramped)
- Clear separation = +1 point (design clarity)

#### **Hallway Scale**
- Hallway should feel navigable, not claustrophobic
- Furniture shouldn't impede passage
- Narrow hallway (under 5 feet) with large furniture = -2 points (tunnel-like)

---

### Vertical Scale (Height Coordination)

#### **Ceiling Height Considerations**
- Low ceilings (under 8 feet): Use vertical art, tall narrow furniture (raises perception)
- High ceilings (over 10 feet): Use horizontal art, larger furniture (grounds space)

**Scoring:**
- **Vertical/horizontal scale matches ceiling height** = +1 point (shows design knowledge)
- **Ignores ceiling height** (horizontal furniture under 8-foot ceiling) = -1 point (visual imbalance)

#### **Furniture Height Coordination**
- Mix of heights creates visual interest
- All low furniture (under 2 feet) = flat, boring = -1 point
- All tall furniture (over 4 feet) = top-heavy, cramped = -1 point
- Mix of heights (dining chair + tall cabinet + low table) = +1 point (dynamic, professional)

---

## **8. FLOORING & GROUNDING (HELPFUL)**

### Why Flooring Matters
Flooring is the foundation of a space's emotional tone. Cold bare tile feels institutional; warm wood feels home-like. Area rugs are critical for acoustic dampening, visual warmth, and defining spaces.

---

### Flooring Materials & Psychology

#### **Warm Flooring (Positive)**

**Hardwood (Real or High-Quality Laminate):**
- Psychology: Warmth, nature, quality, home-like
- Scoring: Real hardwood = +1 point (premium feel)
- Staging: Visible grain, warm tones enhance coziness
- Best for: Living rooms, bedrooms, entryways

**Cork:**
- Psychology: Eco-friendly, warm, soft underfoot
- Scoring: +1 point (shows sustainability, warmth)
- Benefit: Natural acoustic dampening

**Warm Tile or Terracotta:**
- Psychology: Warm, earthy, Mediterranean, grounded
- Scoring: +1 point (warm, sophisticated)
- Best for: Kitchens, entryways (pairs with warm-toned area rug)

#### **Cold Flooring (Neutral to Negative)**

**Concrete (Unfinished):**
- Psychology: Industrial, cold, warehouse, institutional
- Scoring: No penalty if polished + styled; -1 point if bare/gray
- **Fix:** Concrete + large area rug = warms perception significantly (+1 point total)

**Cold Gray/Dark Tile:**
- Psychology: Cold, sterile, hospital-like (especially bathrooms)
- Scoring: No penalty in bathroom; -1 point in living areas if no rug

**Laminate (Cheap, Plastic-y Feel):**
- Psychology: Budget, cheap, synthetic
- Scoring: No penalty if high-quality (looks like wood); -1 point if obviously plastic

**Vinyl Plank (Budget):**
- Psychology: Temporary, rental, budget
- Scoring: No penalty if high-quality; -1 point if obviously synthetic

---

### Area Rug Strategy & Grounding

#### **Rug Size & Placement**

**Living Room Rug:**
- Should ground seating area (sofa front legs on rug, ideally)
- Size: 5x7 (minimum) to 8x10 (optimal for most living rooms)
- Too small (3x5) = floating, scattered = -2 points
- Too large (9x12 in small room) = overwhelming = -1 point
- Proper size = +1 point (grounds space, anchors furniture)

**Bedroom Rug:**
- Should extend from one side of bed (not just a bedside rug)
- Size: 5x7 or 5x8 (typical)
- Ideally, guest feet touch rug when getting out of bed (comfort signal)
- Missing in bedroom = -1 to -2 points (cold floor, institutional)
- Proper placement = +1 point (warmth, comfort)

**Dining Rug:**
- Should be large enough for table + chairs (chairs on rug when pushed in)
- Too small = table floats, scattered = -1 point
- Proper size = +1 point (defines dining zone)

#### **Rug Color & Grounding**

**Dark Rug (Navy, Charcoal, Deep Gray):**
- Anchors light walls (prevents "floating" feeling)
- Grounds space, makes it feel intentional
- Scoring: +1 point (grounding)

**Light Rug (Cream, Beige, Light Gray):**
- Opens space, makes small rooms feel larger
- Risk: Shows dirt, vacuuming visible (high maintenance signal)
- Scoring: +1 point in small spaces; neutral in large spaces

**Pattern Rug (Geometric, Tribal, Abstract):**
- Adds visual interest, personality
- Grounds space if pattern is intentional (matches palette)
- Random pattern = -1 point (looks accidental)
- Cohesive pattern = +1 point (shows curation)

**Rug Texture:**
- High-pile (shag, plush) = cozy, warmth signal = +1 point (hygge)
- Low-pile (flatweave, sisal) = clean, practical = no penalty
- Jute/natural fiber = premium, eco-signal = +1 point

## **9. WINDOW TREATMENTS & LIGHT CONTROL (HELPFUL)**

### Why Window Treatments Matter
Windows are guest's connection to outside world. Poor treatments = privacy violation, light control loss, guest anxiety. Good treatments = guest comfort, flexibility, intentional design.

---

### Curtain Material & Quality

#### **Premium Curtain Fabric**

**Linen or Linen Blend:**
- Feel: Soft, natural, premium, slightly textured
- Psychology: Luxury, sophistication, quality
- Light filtering: Dappled, warm
- Scoring: +1 to +2 points (luxury, natural, intentional)

**Heavy Cotton Canvas:**
- Feel: Substantial, quality, durable
- Psychology: Quality, intentional, professional
- Light filtering: Good blocking (if lined)
- Scoring: +1 point (quality signal)

**Silk or Silk-Blend (Rare):**
- Feel: Luxurious, elegant, reflective
- Psychology: Luxury, formality, high-end
- Scoring: +2 points (premium luxury signal)

#### **Budget Fabrics**

**Thin Polyester:**
- Feel: Plastic-y, cheap, synthetic
- Psychology: Budget hotel, temporary
- Scoring: -1 point (feels institutional)

**Flimsy Cotton (Under 200gsm):**
- Feel: Thin, see-through, cheap
- Psychology: Budget, careless
- Scoring: -1 point (cheap feeling)

---

### Curtain Height & Proportion

#### **Full-Length Curtains (Floor to Ceiling)**
- Dramatic, intentional, makes room feel taller
- Rods mounted at ceiling height (not below crown molding)
- Scoring: +1 point (dramatic, professional, intentional)

#### **Short Curtains (Below Window)**
- Can work but feels less dramatic
- Risk: Makes ceiling feel lower, feels cramped
- Scoring: No penalty if intentional; -1 point if awkward/unfinished-looking

#### **Rod Placement**
- Ceiling-mounted = tallest possible = room feels taller = +1 point
- Below crown molding = -1 point (feels cramped, unfinished)
- Off-center or awkward = -1 point (looks accidental)

---

### Functional Window Treatments

#### **Layering Strategy (Optimal)**
- **Sheer curtain** (light filtering, privacy without darkness)
- **Blackout curtain** (complete darkness, sleep support)
- Guest control = +2 points (luxury, flexibility, guest comfort)

#### **Single Layer**
- Blackout only = functional but less flexible = +1 point
- Sheer only = privacy compromised = no penalty, but limiting

#### **Light Control**
- Guest should be able to control light (essential for sleep)
- No window treatment in bedroom = -3 points (guest anxiety, institutional)
- Partial coverage (half-window curtains) = -1 point (light control compromised)
- Full coverage with blackout = +1 point (sleep support signal)

---

### Curtain Color & Palette Coordination

#### **DO: Curtain Color Matches Palette**
- Curtains should be primary or secondary color (not random accent)
- Example: Navy curtains in beige + navy + rust room = +1 point (cohesive)

#### **DON'T: Random Curtain Color**
- Bright yellow curtains in cool blue room = -1 point (jarring, accidental)
- Curtains don't match other textiles = -1 point (scattered)

#### **Pattern in Curtains**
- Subtle pattern (tone-on-tone, geometric) = +1 point (adds interest without chaos)
- Bold pattern = +1 point if cohesive, -1 point if random (see "Escape Room" yellow curtains as accident)

---

### Window Treatment Mistakes (Red Flags)

**Bare Windows in Bedroom:**
- Flag: "No window treatment = guest cannot control light or privacy"
- Penalty: -3 points (critical miss, institutional, guest discomfort)

**Flimsy, Cheap Curtains:**
- Flag: "Curtain fabric appears thin/synthetic"
- Penalty: -1 point (cheap feeling, detracts from luxury signal)

**Rods Mounted Below Ceiling:**
- Flag: "Rod placement makes room feel shorter/cramped"
- Penalty: -1 point (visual shortcoming)

**Mismatched or Random Curtains:**
- Flag: "Curtain color/pattern doesn't coordinate with palette"
- Penalty: -1 to -2 points (scattered, accidental-looking)

**Windows with Broken Shades or Damaged Treatments:**
- Flag: "Window treatment broken, stained, or damaged"
- Penalty: -1 to -2 points (unmaintained, guest anxiety)

## **6. HIDDEN FRICTION RED FLAGS (CRITICAL)**

### Bedroom Friction
- **No bedside lamp** = Guest can't read in bed = -5 points (CRITICAL)
- **No curtains** = Guest can't control light = -3 points
- **No area rug** = Cold, echoing, unwelcoming = -2 points
- **Bare nightstand** = No place for phone/water/book = -2 points
- **No dresser/hangers** = Nowhere to store clothes = -2 points
- **No chair/bench** = Nowhere to sit while dressing = -1 point

### Living Room Friction
- **No side tables** = Guests have nowhere to set drinks/book = -2 points
- **TV corner-placed** = Awkward viewing angle = -2 points
- **No secondary seating** = Limited gathering options = -1 point
- **Furniture crowding** = Cramped, claustrophobic = -2 points

### Layout Friction
- **Fridge in wrong zone** = Confusing, poor layout = -3 points
- **No defined dining** = No gathering space = -3 points
- **Appliances visible from bedroom** = No privacy boundaries = -2 points
- **Sofa extends into kitchen** = No zone separation = -2 points

### Scoring Rule
- Each friction point = -1 to -3 points (depending on severity)
- Multiple frictions compound (e.g., no lamp + no rug + no curtains = -10 total)
- Identify friction early in report: "This bedroom lacks [X], which creates [Guest Frustration]"

## **7. PRICE-ADJUSTED EXPECTATIONS (CRITICAL)**

Scoring is relative to nightly rate. Adjust Five Pillars weighting based on guest expectations:

### $60–80/night (Budget)
- Guest expectations: Clean + functional (design is bonus)
- Scoring threshold: 60/100 is acceptable
- Design gaps forgiven if cleanliness/functionality excellent
- **Weight:** Function 40%, Cleanliness 40%, Design 20%

### $80–120/night (Mid-Tier)
- Guest expectations: Clean + functional + some personality
- Scoring threshold: 70/100 is baseline, 80/100 is excellent
- Design gaps more noticeable
- **Weight:** Function 30%, Cleanliness 30%, Design 40%

### $120–180/night (Upper-Mid)
- Guest expectations: Curated design + intentionality + above-average comfort
- Scoring threshold: 75/100 is baseline, 85/100 is excellent
- Design gaps = dealbreaker (e.g., no bedside lamps in bedroom)
- **Weight:** Function 20%, Cleanliness 20%, Design 60%

### $180+/night (Luxury)
- Guest expectations: Luxury + expert curation + seamless experience
- Scoring threshold: 80/100 is baseline, 90/100 is excellent
- Any design gap = significant issue
- **Weight:** Function 10%, Cleanliness 10%, Design 80%

### Scoring Rule
- **At $121/night with bare-bones bedrooms** = underperforming (-8 points vs. expectations)
- **At $60/night with same bare-bones bedrooms** = acceptable (-2 points)
- **Adjust Five Pillars weighting** based on price point before final calculation

## **8. OVERALL SCORING SUMMARY**

### Five Pillars (20 points each = 100 total)

**1. Color Coherence (20 points)**
- ✅ +1 to +2: Proper 60-30-10 ratio
- ✅ +1 to +2: Intentional color harmony
- ❌ -1 to -2: Palette mismatch (e.g., lavender chair in neutral room)
- ❌ -3 to -5: Random colors, no strategy

**2. Lighting Quality (20 points)**
- ✅ +1 to +2: Layered (overhead + task + ambient)
- ✅ +1: Warm bulbs (2700K) throughout
- ❌ -2 to -3: Overhead light only (institutional)
- ❌ -3 to -5: No bedside lamps in bedrooms (CRITICAL)
- ❌ -2: Cold light (4000K+ only)

**3. Functional Anchors (20 points)**
- ✅ +1 to +2: Nightstands, bedside lamps, side tables, workspace
- ✅ +1: Secondary seating, gathering spaces
- ❌ -2 to -3: Missing bedside tables/lamps
- ❌ -2 to -3: TV placement dysfunctional
- ❌ -2: No secondary seating

**4. Clutter & Space Flow (20 points)**
- ✅ +1 to +2: Clean, organized, good spatial flow
- ✅ +1: Defined zones (kitchen separate from living room)
- ❌ -2 to -3: Furniture crowding (sofa in kitchen)
- ❌ -3: Fridge in living room or other major layout dysfunction
- ❌ -3: No defined dining/gathering space

**5. Staging Integrity (20 points)**
- ✅ +1 to +3: Art properly placed, styled surfaces, textiles visible
- ✅ +1 to +2: Welcome signals (basket, flowers, personal touches)
- ❌ -1 to -2: Artwork too high, random, or misscaled
- ❌ -1: Professional oversights (toilet seat up)
- ❌ -2 to -3: Bare-bones styling, no textiles, institutional feel

### Weighting (25/20/20/18/17)
- Color Coherence: 25%
- Lighting Quality: 20%
- Functional Anchors: 20%
- Clutter & Space Flow: 18%
- Staging Integrity: 17%

**Final Score = (Color × 0.25) + (Lighting × 0.20) + (Anchors × 0.20) + (Clutter × 0.18) + (Staging × 0.17)**
